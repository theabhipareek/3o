Transport in Plants

Introduction to Plant Transport Systems

Plants need transport systems to move substances to and from their cells. Unlike animals, plants do not have a circulatory system with a pump. Instead, they rely on physical processes to move water, minerals, and food throughout the plant body.

i
Key Learning Objectives
  • Understand the structure and function of xylem and phloem tissues
  • Explain the processes of water uptake and movement in plants
  • Describe the mechanism of translocation in phloem
  • Connect transport processes to plant survival and growth

The two main transport tissues in plants are xylem and phloem. These specialized tissues form continuous systems that extend throughout the plant, connecting the roots, stem, and leaves.

Xylem: Structure and Function

Xylem is responsible for transporting water and dissolved mineral ions from the roots to the leaves. It consists of several specialized cell types that work together to efficiently move water upward against gravity.

X
Xylem Cell Types

Xylem Vessels

Xylem vessels are formed from vessel elements that have died and lost their end walls, creating long, hollow tubes. The cell walls are reinforced with lignin, making them strong enough to withstand the negative pressure created during water transport.

Tracheids

Tracheids are elongated cells with tapered ends and thickened, lignified cell walls. Water moves through pits (thin areas) in the cell walls from one tracheid to another.

Fig 1: Cross-section of xylem tissue showing vessels (large circular structures) and tracheids (smaller cells with thickened walls).

Functions of Xylem

  • Water transport: Moves water from roots to leaves
  • Mineral transport: Carries dissolved minerals to all parts of the plant
  • Support: Lignified cell walls provide mechanical strength to the plant
!
Important Concept

Xylem is a one-way transport system that moves substances upward from roots to leaves. The cells that form xylem vessels are dead at maturity, with only their cell walls remaining.

Water Movement in Plants

Water moves through plants by several mechanisms working together. Understanding these processes is essential for explaining how tall trees can transport water to their uppermost leaves.

Osmosis and Root Pressure

Water enters the plant roots by osmosis. The concentration of mineral ions is kept higher in root cells than in soil water, creating an osmotic gradient that draws water into the root.

?
The Pathway of Water

Water moves from the soil into the root through either:

  • Apoplast pathway: Water moves through cell walls and intercellular spaces
  • Symplast pathway: Water moves through the cytoplasm of cells, connected by plasmodesmata

Once water reaches the endodermis, the Casparian strip forces water to move through the cytoplasm, allowing the plant to control which minerals enter the xylem.

Transpiration-Pull Theory

The main mechanism driving water movement in tall plants is the transpiration-pull (also called the cohesion-tension theory). This process involves:

  1. Transpiration: Water evaporates from the leaves through stomata
  2. Cohesion: Water molecules stick together due to hydrogen bonding
  3. Adhesion: Water molecules stick to the walls of xylem vessels
  4. Tension: A continuous column of water is pulled upward as water evaporates from leaves
Fig 2: Diagram illustrating the transpiration-pull mechanism, showing how water moves from roots to leaves through the xylem.
?
Common Misconception

Many students think plants "suck up" water like drinking through a straw. However, plants don't actively "pull" water—rather, it's the physical properties of water and the structure of xylem vessels that allow water to be drawn upward as it evaporates from leaves.

Factors Affecting Transpiration

The rate of transpiration varies depending on environmental conditions and plant factors. Understanding these factors helps explain plant adaptations to different environments.

Factor Effect on Transpiration Explanation
Light intensity Increases transpiration Stomata open in light for photosynthesis
Temperature Increases transpiration Higher temperatures increase evaporation rate
Humidity Decreases transpiration Less evaporation when air is already moisture-saturated
Wind speed Increases transpiration Wind removes water vapor, maintaining concentration gradient
Available water Increases transpiration More water uptake leads to more transpiration

Plant Adaptations to Control Water Loss

Plants have evolved various adaptations to regulate water loss through transpiration:

  • Stomatal control: Opening and closing stomata to regulate gas exchange and water loss
  • Leaf surface area: Smaller or fewer leaves reduce the surface for transpiration
  • Cuticle: Waxy layer that reduces water loss from the leaf surface
  • Leaf hairs: Create a boundary layer of still air to reduce water loss

Phloem and Translocation

While xylem transports water and minerals upward, phloem is responsible for transporting organic nutrients (mainly sucrose) throughout the plant. This process is called translocation.

P
Phloem Structure

Phloem tissue consists of:

  • Sieve tube elements: Living cells that form tubes for transport
  • Companion cells: Provide metabolic support to sieve tube elements
  • Phloem parenchyma: Storage cells
  • Phloem fibers: Provide structural support
Fig 3: Structure of phloem tissue showing sieve tube elements and companion cells.

Pressure Flow Hypothesis

The mechanism of translocation in phloem is explained by the pressure flow hypothesis:

  1. Loading: Active transport of sucrose into phloem at source (where sugars are produced)
  2. Water follows: Water enters by osmosis, creating high hydrostatic pressure
  3. Mass flow: Solution flows from high pressure (source) to low pressure (sink)
  4. Unloading: Sucrose is removed at sink (where sugars are used/stored)
  5. Water exits: Water leaves by osmosis, maintaining the pressure gradient
i
Key Difference from Xylem

Unlike xylem transport, which is unidirectional (roots to leaves), phloem transport is bidirectional. Organic compounds can be transported from leaves to roots, fruits, or growing points depending on the plant's needs.

Review Flashcards

Test your understanding of key concepts with these flashcards.

What is the main function of xylem tissue?
Xylem transports water and dissolved mineral ions from the roots to the leaves. It also provides structural support to the plant due to its lignified cell walls.
Explain the transpiration-pull mechanism.
The transpiration-pull mechanism (cohesion-tension theory) explains how water moves up through the xylem. As water evaporates from leaves (transpiration), it creates a tension that pulls the water column up. This works because water molecules cohere to each other (cohesion) and adhere to the xylem vessel walls (adhesion), creating a continuous water column from roots to leaves.
How does phloem transport differ from xylem transport?
Key differences include:
  • Xylem transports water and minerals upward, while phloem transports organic nutrients (mainly sucrose) bidirectionally
  • Xylem vessels are dead at maturity, while phloem sieve tubes are living cells
  • Xylem transport is passive (driven by transpiration), while phloem transport requires energy (active loading of sucrose)
  • Xylem uses negative pressure (tension), while phloem uses positive pressure differentials
